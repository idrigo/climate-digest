# Europe's heatwaves just became a grid crisis

Four consecutive heat domes have gripped Europe since late May, and this week the story shifted from a public-health emergency to a grid-security one: France pulled 6.3 GW of nuclear offline as river temperatures breached legal cooling limits, just as the U.S. National Academies published its most authoritative attribution report in a decade — under direct political pressure to bury it. Meanwhile, solar supplied a quarter of the EU's electricity in June for the first time, a milestone for the build-out meant to buffer these shocks. The throughline for weather, climate, and energy practitioners this week: extremes are no longer just a forecasting problem — they're an infrastructure problem.

## The Big Story

EuroMOMO data released this week confirmed 10,650 excess deaths across 27 European countries in a single peak week (June 22–28) — the worst week of heat mortality on record, with Belgium logging its highest heatwave toll since tracking began in 2000. World Weather Attribution called the event "virtually impossible" without human-caused warming. Days later, the National Academies released its first attribution-science update in a decade, affirming climate change has "a direct and well-understood impact" on extreme heat and rainfall — publishing it despite Republican senators alleging author bias, an opposition research firm filing records requests against panel members, and a Senate bill aimed at keeping such findings out of climate liability courtrooms.

The more consequential development for practitioners is what the heat is doing to the grid. France has curtailed or shut eleven nuclear reactors — three fully offline — removing roughly 14% of national demand at the exact moment cooling load peaks, because river water is too warm to legally absorb the plants' waste heat. An emergency exemption for the Bugey plant expires July 20, forcing grid operators into the same call they faced in 2022: seek another exemption or take more capacity offline mid-heatwave. It's the clearest evidence yet that "firm, dispatchable" baseload assumptions break down precisely when demand peaks — and exactly the failure mode battery storage and demand-flexibility markets are now being priced to solve (see Europe's negative-price surge below).

## Research Corner

Harvard Forest's 37-year soil warming experiment — the longest-running study of its kind — found that sustained warming causes microbes to decompose soil carbon once assumed stable, a feedback invisible on short timescales and only apparent after decades of observation. Researchers are calling for it to be built into the next generation of climate models, which would push upper-bound warming projections higher. Anyone citing climate projections should note that current models likely underestimate long-run carbon-cycle feedbacks. (https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260713000809.htm)
