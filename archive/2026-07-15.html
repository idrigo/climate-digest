Climate

The headline scientific finding of the past 72 hours comes from Harvard Forest, where results from the world's longest-running soil warming experiment — now in its 37th year — show that microbes can decompose supposedly stable humic soil carbon under warming conditions, a process that could strengthen positive climate feedbacks and push future warming projections higher than current models indicate. No new C3S or CAMS bulletins have been published in this window; the June 2026 baselines from those services (June ranked second-warmest June globally at +1.39°C; 82% of the ocean in marine heatwave conditions) established in the July 14 digest remain operative.

  • ScienceDaily / Sci. Total Environ. 37-year Harvard Forest soil warming experiment reveals hidden climate feedback — warming causes microbes to break down stable (humic) soil carbon once thought largely protected; process strengthens warming–carbon-release feedback loop; finding not apparent in short-term experiments, underscoring value of decadal-scale observation; incorporation into climate models expected to raise upper-bound warming projections Published July 13, 2026 (ScienceDaily reporting on research published in Science of The Total Environment; led by Jerry Melillo, Marine Biological Laboratory): The Harvard Forest experiment — begun in 1991 and now the oldest continuous soil-warming study in the world — uses buried heating rods to maintain experimental plots at 5°C above ambient temperature, closely mimicking the projected warming trajectory for temperate forest soils in New England by mid-century. The finding that this warming causes microbial communities to access and decompose forms of soil organic matter previously classified as stable (mineral-associated and humic fractions) overturns an assumption embedded in most earth system models: that labile (easily decomposable) carbon would be depleted within the first few decades of warming, after which soil carbon losses would plateau. Instead, the 37-year record shows no plateau — decomposition of stable fractions has accelerated as the experiment has continued, with the most recent decade showing the largest rate of change. Soils globally store an estimated 1,500–2,400 GtC, roughly twice the amount of carbon in the atmosphere; even a fractional release of the stable fraction under warming represents a forcing comparable to years of human emissions. The researchers note that short-term experiments (1–5 years) would have completely missed this signal, and call for updated parameterisation of soil carbon decomposition in CMIP7 models ahead of the next IPCC assessment cycle.

  • Eastern Pacific — TROPICAL DEPRESSION FIVE-E FORMING; FORECAST TO REACH HURRICANE INTENSITY BY THURSDAY DEVELOPING — NO COASTAL WATCHES/WARNINGS CURRENTLY: Tropical Depression Five-E formed in the eastern Pacific approximately 560 miles south-southwest of the southern tip of Baja California as of July 14-15. As of 2100 UTC July 14, the system was located near 14.8°N, 109.2°W, moving west-northwest at approximately 18 mph with maximum sustained winds of 35 mph (55 km/h). The NHC forecast projects steady strengthening over the coming days, with the depression expected to reach tropical storm intensity (becoming Tropical Storm Emilia) followed by further intensification to hurricane strength by Thursday, July 17. No coastal watches or warnings are in effect and the storm is expected to remain well offshore Mexico. The rapid formation and expected intensification of TD Five-E is consistent with the unusually warm eastern Pacific sea surface temperatures that have characterised the 2026 season, which is running well ahead of the historical average at this point in the season. The eastern Pacific hurricane season tends to be most active during a developing El Niño — in contrast to the suppressed Atlantic — and the 2026 Pacific season has already produced multiple named storms. Five-E's forecast track carrying it west-northwest and away from the Mexican coast is the typical trajectory for early-season eastern Pacific systems that form in that latitude band; the primary hazard is swell on southwestern Mexican beaches and surf along the Baja California coast.

  • Euronews / Egypt Oil & Gas France shuts down or curtails 11 nuclear reactors, removing 6.3 GW (~14% of national demand) as heatwave raises river temperatures above legal discharge limits — three units fully offline (Golfech, Bugey, Chooz); emergency exemption granted to Bugey to discharge warmer water into Rhône through July 20; energy security warnings issued across UK, Germany, Switzerland as France's export capacity shrinks during peak European cooling demand Published July 12–13, 2026 (Euronews July 13; Egypt Oil & Gas; TechTimes July 13; Travel and Tour World; nomadlawyer.org): France operates 56 nuclear reactors providing approximately 70% of its electricity under normal conditions and is the EU's largest net electricity exporter. French nuclear plants use river water for cooling and are legally required to return that water below regulatory temperature thresholds to protect river ecosystems (particularly oxygen levels and aquatic life at downstream fish habitats). During a heatwave, river water is already warm; plants cannot discharge additional heated water without exceeding ecological limits. The three fully offline units — Golfech Unit 2 (1,300 MW), Bugey Unit 3 (900 MW), and Chooz Unit 2 (1,450 MW) — represent approximately 3,650 MW of hard capacity loss; the additional partial curtailments across eight more units bring the total reduction to ~6.3 GW. The emergency exemption granted to Bugey (allowing warmer-than-permitted discharge into the Rhône through July 20) follows a precedent from the 2022 European heatwave when similar exemptions were granted to multiple French nuclear plants, and was explicitly justified by grid security grounds — the same justification used in 2022 when France's export balance briefly turned negative. The UK, Germany, and Switzerland grid operators have all flagged reduced import availability from France as a supply-side constraint in their mid-July grid adequacy assessments.

Renewables

Two policy dynamics converged on July 15. Today marks the European Commission's internal deadline to assess whether alternative (non-Chinese) inverter suppliers can meet EU solar demand, a finding that will determine whether the April funding ban proceeds as written or triggers a transitional accommodation — with Wood Mackenzie estimating 28+ GW of European solar capacity at risk through 2030 if Chinese sourcing is fully blocked without a viable European alternative confirmed. Separately, France's July 12–14 nuclear curtailment — 6.3 GW removed from the grid — exposed the gap that intermittent heat-stressed generation creates in even the most nuclear-heavy grid in Europe, putting battery storage and demand flexibility front and centre for European energy system operators.

This assessment is not merely administrative: its finding directly shapes whether the Commission issues updated guidance narrowing the ban, extends the transitional period beyond November 1, 2026, or proceeds with the full implementation that SolarPower Europe and Wood Mackenzie have warned could freeze project pipelines. The 28 GW figure — representing approximately 14% of forecast European solar PV demand through 2030 — is a market disruption equivalent to roughly 1.5–2 years of German solar installation at 2025 pace. Central and Eastern European markets are most exposed because they have the highest proportions of Chinese-sourced inverters in existing and contracted project pipelines, and the lowest domestic inverter manufacturing capacity; a November 1 hard cutoff without guidance clarity could freeze financing decisions across the entire CEE renewable project pipeline. The solar sector's lobbying group SolarPower Europe called the current lack of written guidance "a freezing of financing decisions" in its July 14 statement (covered in the July 14 digest); today's Commission assessment outcome is the critical next event that will determine whether that freeze extends or resolves.
  • Euronews / Egypt Oil & Gas France's 6.3 GW nuclear curtailment during heatwave exposes European grid fragility — 14% of French demand supply removed during peak cooling; emergency river-temperature exemption granted to Bugey plant through July 20; UK, Germany, Switzerland warned of reduced French electricity export capacity; episode highlights need for grid-scale storage and demand flexibility as complement to nuclear baseload during heat extremes Published July 12–14, 2026 (Egypt Oil & Gas; Euronews July 13; TechTimes July 13; nomadlawyer.org; TFTC): The French nuclear curtailment episode is the third major demonstration in four years — after the 2022 and 2024 European heatwaves produced similar reactor output cuts — that nuclear plants relying on river cooling are structurally vulnerable to the specific kind of sustained high temperatures Europe is experiencing in summer 2026. The 2022 episode was arguably more severe (France lost approximately 17 GW of nuclear capacity at peak and briefly became a net electricity importer for the first time in decades); the 2026 event, at 6.3 GW, is smaller but arrives in a summer with less grid slack — Germany's nuclear exit is complete, interconnector capacity is constrained by ongoing heatwave demand across the continent, and the battery storage buildout in France has not yet reached a scale that can meaningfully bridge multi-GW gaps. The episode is being cited by European energy regulators, battery storage developers, and demand-response aggregators as the clearest recent evidence for the "dispatchability premium" argument: the ability to dispatch clean power during extreme heat events, when thermal power generation (including nuclear) is capacity-constrained and solar output is high but grid-frequency volatility is elevated, is the primary market signal now driving European battery storage investment. France's Bugey emergency exemption — allowing discharge above ecological limits to maintain grid security — also creates a political and legal flashpoint: environmental groups have already filed or are expected to file challenges to the exemption on aquatic ecosystem protection grounds.

Industry News

The headline deal in the July 12–15 window is Masdar's July 13 announcement of a $6.1 billion financial close for a 5.2 GW solar plus 19 GWh battery storage project in Abu Dhabi — the world's first gigascale 24/7 renewable energy project, designed to deliver continuous clean electricity without fossil fuel backup and representing the single largest solar-plus-storage financing in history.

Masdar Closes $6.1 Billion Financing for World's First Gigascale 24/7 Renewable Energy Project — 5.2 GW Solar Plus 19 GWh Battery Storage in Abu Dhabi

Abu Dhabi's state renewable energy company Masdar announced on July 13 the financial close of a $6.1 billion project combining a 5.2 GW solar photovoltaic plant with a 19 GWh battery energy storage system in Abu Dhabi — the largest solar-plus-storage financing ever completed and the world's first demonstration at "gigascale" that round-the-clock clean power delivery is commercially financeable without fossil fuel backup. The project, co-developed with state-owned Emirates Water and Electricity Company (EWEC), was funded through a $5.1 billion debt package from a consortium of 13 banks spanning the UAE, France, the UK, Germany, China, and Japan, with Masdar contributing $1 billion in equity. Once operational in 2027, the plant will deliver 1 GW of continuous clean electricity to the Abu Dhabi grid regardless of time of day or weather — effectively making it dispatchable in the same operational sense as a natural gas peaker plant, but without emissions. The deal is a landmark for bankability: the 13-bank consortium's participation across multiple jurisdictions signals that project finance lenders have crossed a threshold of confidence in large-scale storage's ability to guarantee the kind of firm, predictable output that debt covenants require. For Masdar, the close advances its target of 100 GW of renewable capacity by 2030 from its current 65 GW deployed base.
