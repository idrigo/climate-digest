Industry News

Tesla Posts Record-Near Q2 2026 Energy Storage Quarter: 13.5 GWh Deployed, Cumulative Total Tops 132 GWh

Tesla reported on July 2 that it deployed 13.5 gigawatt-hours of energy storage in Q2 2026 — a 53% sequential increase over Q1's 8.8 GWh and the company's second-largest quarterly deployment total ever, just short of the 14.2 GWh record set in Q4 2025. The figure brings Tesla's cumulative Megapack and Powerwall deployment total past 132 GWh since 2016, a milestone that arrives as the US grid is operating under a Department of Energy emergency order authorising data-centre curtailment during the heat dome. For scale: 13.5 GWh in a single quarter from one manufacturer exceeds what the entire US utility-scale energy storage market deployed annually as recently as 2022, illustrating the sector's compound growth curve. Tesla also announced its Q2 2026 financial results will be presented in an earnings webcast on July 22.

SAEL Industries Breaks Ground on $954 Million 10 GW Integrated Solar Manufacturing Complex in India — One of South Asia's Largest Clean Energy Capital Commitments

Indian renewable developer SAEL Industries held a groundbreaking ceremony on June 29 for an integrated solar manufacturing facility in Jewar, Uttar Pradesh, committing ₹82 billion ($954 million) to build 5 GW of TOPCon solar cell manufacturing capacity and 5 GW of module capacity on a 200-acre site in the Yamuna Expressway Industrial Development Authority zone. Once operational, the facility will bring SAEL's total solar manufacturing capacity to 8.5 GW and create approximately 20,000 local jobs. The project is structured under India's Production Linked Incentive scheme and is positioned to supply both domestic Indian demand and export markets as global module procurement diversifies away from Chinese supply chains — an accelerating shift driven by FEOC restrictions in the US, EU tariffs on Chinese solar equipment, and mounting geopolitical risk in Chinese-dominated supply chains.

NS Energy

DOE Emergency Orders Force AI Data Centres onto Backup Generators During Heat Wave — A Structural Inflection Point for Grid Regulation

The US Department of Energy invoked Section 202(c) of the Federal Power Act on June 30, issuing two emergency orders authorising PJM Interconnection to direct data centres and other industrial loads of 50 MW or more — predominantly AI computing facilities concentrated in Virginia, Maryland, and Delaware — to switch to their own backup generators within 15 minutes of an emergency signal. Both orders ran through July 3 and also waived environmental operating restrictions on power plants to expand available supply. PJM simultaneously projected a potential all-time demand record of 166,304 MW, and wholesale electricity prices spiked above $1,600/MWh during evening transmission congestion — from under $40/MWh earlier in the same day. The emergency orders represent the sharpest demonstration to date of the structural tension between exponential AI data-centre electricity demand — which has added tens of gigawatts to Mid-Atlantic grid load in three years — and grid infrastructure not designed for it; they are widely expected to accelerate FERC rulemaking on large-load interconnection disclosure requirements and mandatory demand-response obligations for hyperscale consumers.

The Hill

July 4 Is the Last Day US Wind and Solar Projects Can Begin Construction to Preserve IRA-Era Tax Credits Under the OBBBA

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act's construction deadline — July 4, 2026 — is now effectively at hand: after today, wind and solar facilities that have not demonstrably begun physical construction lose eligibility for the 45Y clean energy production tax credit and 48E clean energy investment tax credit, unless they are placed in service by December 31, 2027. A federal court ruling on June 6 reinstated the 5% safe harbor as a valid pathway (vacating IRS Notice 2025-42), but the government may appeal and developers face residual legal exposure in relying on it. The cut-off is wind-and-solar specific: other zero-emission technologies — geothermal, nuclear, storage — retain credit eligibility through 2032–2035 under a separate phasedown schedule. Industry analysts estimate that multiple gigawatts of US wind and solar capacity in early development stages, relying on IRA economics conceived before the OBBBA, will face fundamental reconfiguration of their return profiles — a structural headwind that is expected to slow the US renewable pipeline over 2026–2028 even as storage, geothermal, and nuclear see continued policy support.
