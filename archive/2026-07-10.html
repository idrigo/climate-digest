Climate

The July 8–10 window produced two significant policy-layer developments extending beyond the established WWA/C3S attribution science from June 2026. The EU Parliament's lead rapporteur on ETS Market Stability Reserve reform opted July 9 not to advance the standalone MSR amendment, folding it into the comprehensive ETS overhaul now set for July 17 — a move that makes the reform simultaneously wider in scope and more politically complex, arriving as EU carbon allowances trade near their highest level since February 2026. Separately, China's State Council published its "Beautiful China" five-year action plan for 2026–2030 on July 3, confirmed by the Carbon Brief China Briefing on July 9: the plan sets binding targets including carbon peaking on schedule, a 3% carbon intensity reduction for ETS-covered industries by 2030, and a clean-energy mandate for all new electricity demand — a significant diplomatic signal ahead of COP30 in Brazil.

  • Carbon Pulse EU Parliament puts ETS Market Stability Reserve reform on hold — lead rapporteur opts not to advance standalone MSR amendment, citing need to assess it in the context of the comprehensive July 17 ETS overhaul; scope confirmed to include waste sector, extra-European flights, CDR integration, and CBAM interaction Published July 9, 2026: The European Parliament's lead lawmaker on the ETS Market Stability Reserve revision announced on July 9 that she would not advance the standalone MSR amendment, preferring instead to fold it into the broader ETS overhaul package the European Commission is due to publish on July 17. The decision reflects concerns that moving the MSR — the mechanism that controls how allowances enter and exit the EU carbon market, and therefore the primary lever over long-run carbon price trajectory — in isolation from the rest of the ETS reform package risks incoherence: the July 17 review is confirmed to address not only the linear reduction factor and free-allowance benchmarks (as reported July 8 by Bloomberg), but also the expansion of ETS scope to cover the waste sector and extra-European aviation, integration of permanent carbon dioxide removals into the trading framework, new rules on non-permanent carbon capture and utilisation, and the interaction between free allowances and the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism. The EU ETS carbon price is currently near its highest levels since February 2026, reflecting both tight 2026 supply (approximately 8% fewer allowances than 2025) and pre-reform positioning uncertainty; the Parliament's decision to wait for the July 17 package before moving on the MSR introduces an additional two-to-three week delay in resolving the market's structural uncertainty.
  • Xinhua / Carbon Brief China's State Council issues "Beautiful China" 2026–2030 action plan — targets include carbon peaking on schedule, 3% carbon intensity cut for ETS-covered industries by 2030, clean energy to meet all new electricity demand, controlling coal capacity while accelerating retirement Published July 3, 2026 (Xinhua); covered in Carbon Brief China Briefing July 9, 2026: China's State Council published the "Beautiful China" action plan, a comprehensive five-year environmental and climate framework covering 2026–2030 — the 15th Five-Year Plan period. The plan sets out seven key environmental task areas, with climate-specific commitments including: carbon peaking "on schedule" (China's NDC target is peak emissions before 2030); a 3% reduction in carbon emissions per unit of production at industries participating in China's national emissions trading system; "controlling and reducing" coal use with "reasonably controlled" coal-power capacity (a formulation that analysts note leaves significant ambiguity about timing and scale); and a requirement that all new electricity demand be met by clean energy. China's national ETS, which covers the power sector and is expanding to additional industries including steel, aluminium, and cement, recently crossed the equivalent of an EU ETS price threshold that gives its incentive structure real-economy relevance; the "Beautiful China" plan's 3% carbon intensity target is the first time China has tied a specific per-unit ETS intensity target to a five-year plan. The plan was highlighted by Carbon Brief's July 9 China Briefing alongside the ongoing Guangxi flood disaster and a new bilateral EU–China climate implementation mechanism, underscoring the breadth of China-related climate developments in the week's final days.

  • China — Guangxi Flooding from Tropical Storm Maysak — 39 Dead, Dam Breach ONGOING — RESCUE OPERATIONS CONTINUING: Tropical Storm Maysak (separate system from Bavi) made landfall in Guangxi, southern China, triggering catastrophic flooding; 39 confirmed dead, 9 still missing, approximately 130,000 people evacuated as of July 9; Liulan Reservoir's dam at Hengzhou partially collapsed July 7 when cumulative rainfall exceeded design thresholds, killing 26 people in the resulting torrent — the single deadliest individual infrastructure failure of the disaster; 55,000 directly affected across Guangxi with over 1,600 km of roads damaged; secondary hazard: at least 100 animals including alpacas, zebras, and approximately 30 peacocks escaped Guigang Zoo after flooding damaged enclosures; snakes from a nearby farm prompted local authorities to pre-position antivenom stocks; rescue operations still active as of CGTN reporting July 9 Tropical Storm Maysak is a distinct tropical cyclone system from Super Typhoon Bavi, having made landfall in Guangxi approximately one week before Bavi approaches China's Fujian/Zhejiang coast. Maysak's catastrophic rainfall in the Guangxi interior — cumulative totals of 100–400 mm in most areas, exceeding 900 mm in the hardest-hit zones — overwhelmed rivers, reservoirs, and drainage infrastructure across the province. The Liulan Reservoir dam breach in Hengzhou is the defining structural failure of the disaster: the partial collapse of two sections of the dam on the morning of July 7 sent a torrent of water into Hengzhou city, claiming 26 of the disaster's 39 confirmed deaths. Approximately 130,000 people have been evacuated, with drones and boats deployed in an extensive rescue operation to reach communities cut off by floodwaters. Guangxi borders Vietnam and the flood event is being tracked by Vietnam's National Center for Hydro-meteorological Forecasting due to concerns about downstream river flows. The disaster arrives just days before Typhoon Bavi is forecast to make landfall in Fujian/Zhejiang, raising concerns about compound flood risk along China's southeastern coast if ground saturation and river levels from Maysak remnants persist.

Renewables

The passage of the OBBBA's July 4 construction-start deadline has produced a clearer picture of what was the largest rush-to-build in the history of US renewable energy: InsideClimate News' July 9 analysis found the United States is on track to add 42,971 MW of utility-scale solar in 2026 alone — a 45% increase over 2025 and by far the largest single-year solar build ever recorded — as developers scrambled to lock in tax credits before the cut-off. The post-deadline landscape is significantly harsher for projects that missed the July 4 start date: new solar starts must be placed in service by December 31, 2027, a timeline analysts describe as challenging for most project types and near-impossible for many larger utility-scale systems.

  • InsideClimate News Even with tariffs and tax changes, US utility-scale solar on track for 42,971 MW in 2026 — 45% increase over 2025, the largest solar build year ever; 9,070 MW already online Jan–May; 33,901 MW completing June–December; driven by OBBBA construction-start deadline rush; low cost and short timeline sustain momentum Published July 9, 2026: InsideClimate News' "Inside Clean Energy" analysis synthesises EIA planning data and industry tracking to document the scale of the US utility-scale solar construction pipeline for 2026: the 42,971 MW on track for completion this year represents a 45% increase over the already-record 2025 build year and dwarfs any other power generation technology. The January–May 2026 figure of 9,070 MW already online is consistent with a full-year record; the remaining 33,901 MW due for commissioning June–December reflects the concentrated completion of projects that broke ground in 2025 and early 2026 specifically to lock in tax credit eligibility before the OBBBA's July 4, 2026 construction-start cut-off. The analysis notes that despite tariffs on imported solar panels — and despite the OBBBA eliminating the 30%+ investment tax credit for projects starting after July 4 — solar's economics remain compelling: it is the lowest-cost source of new electricity generation in most US markets, and its short lead time (utility-scale projects can commission in 12–18 months, versus 5–10 years for nuclear or large gas) makes it the natural answer to rapidly rising electricity demand from AI data centres and manufacturing reshoring. A companion forward look shows 42,875 MW in the planning pipeline for 2027, suggesting the boom will decelerate modestly but not collapse as the ITC deadline effect fades.
  • PV Magazine USA Qualifying for solar tax credits after the July 4 OBBBA deadline — projects starting after July 4 must now complete by December 31, 2027 to retain any ITC eligibility; Rhodium Group estimates OBBBA will cut clean power capacity build by up to 55% over 2025–2035 vs. baseline; small developers disproportionately affected by compliance-cost burden Published July 8, 2026: PV Magazine USA's post-July 4 analysis details the legal and commercial landscape for US solar projects that did not establish a construction start before the OBBBA's July 4 cut-off date. Projects that began construction after July 4, 2026 face an extremely compressed timeline: they must be placed in service by December 31, 2027 to retain any eligibility for the Section 48E investment tax credit — a requirement that gives large utility-scale projects (typically 24–36 months from start to commission) almost no viable path, while smaller rooftop and community solar systems with shorter timelines may be able to meet the standard. The Five Percent Safe Harbor — allowing a project to establish construction by paying or incurring at least 5% of total costs, followed by continuous efforts to complete — remains available but requires rapid capital commitment. Rhodium Group's modelling suggests the OBBBA will reduce new clean power capacity additions by up to 55% over the 2025–2035 decade relative to a no-OBBBA baseline — primarily through the loss of large-scale wind (whose multiyear lead time makes the deadline nearly impossible to meet for projects not already under construction) and utility-scale solar. The 85% of safe-harboured projects that locked in status before December 2025 will continue their build-out largely unaffected, explaining why the 2026 pipeline remains strong even as the long-term trajectory after 2027 looks significantly more constrained.

One notable development at the edge of the rolling window: Danish climate-tech VC Climentum Capital announced the first close of its new Fund II at €60M toward a €100M target, anchored by a €40M commitment from the European Investment Fund — one of the EIF's largest single-allocation commitments to a climate-focused seed/Series A fund in 2026.

Climentum Capital Closes €60 Million First Round of Fund II — EIF Provides €40 Million Anchor, Targeting Climate Hardware Seed and Series A Across Northern Europe

Danish climate-tech venture capital firm Climentum Capital announced the first close of its second fund at €60 million, against a target fund size of €100 million. The fund is anchored by a €40 million commitment from the European Investment Fund, part of the European Investment Bank Group, alongside Denmark's export and investment fund EIFO and the Danish Society of Engineers (IDA). Climentum Fund II is structured as an Article 9 fund under the EU's Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation — the category reserved exclusively for products whose primary objective is sustainable investment — and operates a dual-carry model that ties manager compensation to both financial returns and verifiable carbon emissions avoided. The fund will focus on Seed and Series A rounds in climate hardware and deeptech companies across Denmark, Sweden, Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, with priority investment themes in energy, heavy industry decarbonisation, sustainable transport, and agriculture; the EIF's €40M anchor commitment is among its largest single allocations to a climate-focused early-stage European fund in 2026 and reflects the EIB Group's stated intention to increase its climate-directed VC exposure in the 15th Five-Year Plan equivalent of EU climate finance toward the COP30 window.
