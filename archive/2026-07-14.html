Climate

Twin bulletins from Copernicus monitoring services published this week provide the most authoritative scientific snapshot of 2026's warming trajectory to date. The C3S climate bulletin confirmed June 2026 as the second-warmest June on record globally, at +1.39°C above pre-industrial levels, with Western Europe posting its hottest June ever at 3.05°C above the 1991–2020 average — a new regional benchmark surpassing even June 2025. Simultaneously, the Copernicus Marine Service documented that 82% of the global ocean was experiencing marine heatwave conditions at the end of June — the second-largest such extent in the observational record — with a new daily global sea surface temperature record set on June 21. Both reports reinforce the accelerating warming trajectory identified by Carbon Brief's mid-year synthesis covered in yesterday's digest.

Renewables

Two policy developments that will materially constrain renewable energy project pipelines crystallized in the July 11–14 window. SolarPower Europe on July 14 issued a formal call to the European Commission for clarity on the scope of its April ban on EU funding for solar and storage projects using inverters from high-risk suppliers — primarily China — warning that the current absence of written guidance is causing project delays and "deep uncertainty" that could affect up to 14% of the EU's solar demand through 2030, equivalent to more than 28 GW of inverter capacity according to Wood Mackenzie. In the United States, the July 4 construction-start deadline embedded in the OBBBA has now passed: new wind and solar projects that did not break ground before July 4 can no longer access the federal Production Tax Credit or Investment Tax Credit under sections 45Y and 48E of the Internal Revenue Code, marking the sharpest single reversal of US clean energy incentive policy in a decade, though storage, geothermal, hydro, and biogas projects are relatively unaffected.

The ban, implemented by the European Commission on May 1, 2026, blocks EU funding (including grants, concessional loans, and contracts from EU institutions) for renewable energy and storage projects using inverters from countries designated as "high-risk" — China, Russia, Iran, and North Korea — with China being the supplier of approximately 60–70% of all solar inverters currently deployed in Europe. European inverter manufacturing capacity is growing (SolarEdge, SMA, Fronius, and others have expanded European production) but is currently estimated to cover only a fraction of EU demand; the timeline to scale European manufacturing to a level that could replace Chinese sourcing is measured in years, not months.

General Fusion Lists on Nasdaq as World's First Publicly Traded Fusion Energy Company — Shares Surge 40% on Debut

Canadian fusion developer General Fusion began trading on Nasdaq on July 13 under the ticker GFUZ, becoming the first pure-play fusion energy company to reach public markets anywhere in the world — beating competitors including Trump-backed TAE Technologies by at least several months. The listing was completed via a business combination with SPAC Spring Valley Acquisition Corp. III, whose shareholders voted in favour of the deal on July 6; the combined company enters the public markets with approximately US$150 million in cash and an initial market capitalisation of around US$1 billion, with shares rising 40% from the US$12.85 reference price in the first hours of trading on July 13. The proceeds will fund General Fusion's Lawson programme through a series of technical milestones in Magnetized Target Fusion targeted for completion in 2028, with the broader commercial objective of demonstrating a cost-competitive fusion energy system for grid integration. The company was recently ranked first on TIME's list of the World's Top GreenTech Companies of 2026. The listing also closes the gap on Fervo Energy (geothermal, $1.9B IPO in May 2026) and Proxima Fusion (which closed €411M in June) as the most watched energy-technology public-market events of the year.

Climate Tech IPO Market Logs Biggest Half-Year Surge Since 2022 — 153 Transactions, $11.6 Billion in Clean-Energy Public Listings

The global climate technology sector completed its busiest first half on record for public listings and acquisitions in H1 2026, with a total of 153 transactions announced between January and June — a 70% increase from the same period in 2025, according to a report by market intelligence firm Currence published by Bloomberg on July 13. At least 10 clean-energy companies went public during the half, raising a combined $11.6 billion — the largest six-month total for the sector since 2022. The standout transaction was geothermal developer Fervo Energy's May 2026 IPO, which raised $1.89 billion in an upsized offering and saw shares pop 33% on Nasdaq debut, making it the largest clean-energy IPO on record. The pipeline of data-centre power supply solutions was a secondary driver: power infrastructure firms targeting AI data centre demand — including low-carbon data-centre operators DayOne ($4.5B) and NScale ($2B) — accounted for a disproportionate share of the capital raised, reflecting hyperscale operators' urgent need for firm, dispatchable clean power. General Fusion's July 13 debut adds the first pure-play fusion listing to an H1 vintage that already includes geothermal, offshore wind, and carbon capture vehicles. Climate tech VC funding separately reached $26.1 billion in H1 2026 (+55% year-on-year), according to CTVC's mid-year review, with low-carbon data-centre infrastructure representing 34% of total invested capital. These figures represent a significant reversal from the 2023–2025 trough in climate tech capital market activity.
