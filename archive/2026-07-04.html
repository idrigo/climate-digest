Renewables

July 4 closes the door on IRA-era clean energy tax credit economics for wind and solar: the OBBBA construction deadline has expired, ending the era in which a new wind or solar project could lock in the 45Y or 48E credit simply by beginning construction. The clean energy finance landscape pivots immediately — storage, geothermal, and nuclear retain credit eligibility through 2032–2035 and are expected to capture capital that previously flowed to wind and solar. Internationally, the UK's AR8 offshore wind CfD auction countdown is now at 16 days; and the July 4 heat dome delivered a high-stakes stress test for renewable-dependent grids that yielded a cautiously optimistic result: batteries and demand response held the line, and no blackouts occurred.

Industry News

National Grid Ventures Invests $1.75 Billion in Joulent to Power AI Data Centres Directly — Bypassing the Congested Grid

National Grid Ventures, the international investment arm of the UK's National Grid, announced a $1.75 billion strategic minority investment in Joulent — a US power developer founded by activist investor Engine No. 1 and GE Vernova specifically to build co-located, off-grid generation assets for AI data centres. Joulent's flagship project, Project Kilby in West Texas, is a 2.67 GW co-located power and transmission facility serving a Microsoft-operated data centre under a 20-year power purchase agreement, with first power delivery targeted for 2028 through a 50/50 joint venture with Chevron Corporation. The deal reflects a structural shift in how hyperscale computing infrastructure is being powered: rather than connecting to an already-congested transmission grid and waiting years for interconnection queue clearance, Joulent co-locates dedicated generation directly at the data-centre site, effectively creating private utility-scale microgrids for the largest AI workloads. For National Grid, the investment is an aggressive pivot into the US energy market, where AI-driven load growth is adding tens of gigawatts of demand at a pace the conventional grid was not designed to absorb.

Tata Motors and Welspun Renewable Energy Sign 86 MW Wind-Solar Hybrid PPA — Indian Automaker Deepens Renewable Manufacturing Commitment

India's Tata Motors signed a long-term Power Purchase Agreement with Welspun Renewable Energy on July 3 to jointly develop an 86 MW wind-solar hybrid renewable energy project that will supply clean electricity directly to the automaker's commercial vehicle manufacturing facility. The deal is structured under India's open-access framework and adds to the growing roster of industrial anchor-tenant PPAs that are driving large-scale renewable project development across India's manufacturing belt. For Welspun — one of India's largest independent renewable power producers — the deal extends a portfolio of gigawatt-scale industrial PPA commitments spanning automotive, cement, and steel customers. The transaction underscores that India's industrial decarbonisation drive is accelerating independently of the US policy uncertainty around the OBBBA, with PPAs for captive renewable consumption increasingly replacing grid electricity purchases for large manufacturers seeking both cost certainty and emissions reduction targets.

Isometric Raises $40 Million Series A to Expand AI-Powered Certification Beyond Carbon Markets Into the Broader Industrial Economy

London-based Isometric, the world's largest certifier of carbon removal by contracted volume, closed a $40 million Series A led by AVP (formerly AXA Venture Partners) — joined by returning investors Lowercarbon Capital and Plural, plus personal investments from Kleiner Perkins chairman John Doerr and investor Walter Kortschak. Isometric's platform deploys AI agents to ingest and cross-check sensor readings, satellite imagery, supply chain records, and laboratory results that underpin a carbon or sustainability certification claim, flagging inconsistencies and escalating edge cases to human experts — a model designed to fix the fundamental credibility problem that collapsed voluntary carbon markets in 2023–2024, when certifiers were paid by the same project developers whose claims they were assessing. The company has contracted to certify more than 16 million tonnes of carbon removal across clients including Microsoft, Anglo American, JPMorganChase, and Boeing, and will use the new capital to expand its Certify platform into a $350 billion global certification market spanning industrial regulatory compliance, safety standards, and broader sustainability requirements — positioning itself as critical infrastructure for the next phase of corporate climate commitments and Article 6 carbon trading under the Paris Agreement.
