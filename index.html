Climate

A World Weather Attribution rapid-attribution study published July 7–8 concluded that Europe's late-June 2026 heatwave would have been "virtually impossible" without fossil-fuel-driven climate change — the extreme temperatures are now 10–100 times more likely than in the pre-industrial era, exposed 327 million people and $15.6 trillion in assets, and contributed to more than 2,025 excess deaths in France alone. In parallel, the EU added a major carbon policy signal on July 8 when Bloomberg reported that the European Commission's July 17 ETS review will offer heavy industry more free allowances and a slower CO₂ reduction pace, while simultaneously launching a new €30 billion ETS Investment Booster fund — a tradeoff that draws the familiar tension between near-term industrial competitiveness and long-term decarbonisation credibility.

  • World Weather Attribution Fossil fuel emissions make Europe's heatwaves 10–100 times more likely — June 2026 event "virtually impossible" without climate change — rapid attribution study published July 7–8; 327 million people and $15.6 trillion in assets exposed; 2,025 excess deaths in France; heat at this level was "virtually unheard of" 50 years ago Published July 7–8, 2026: The World Weather Attribution network — an international research consortium that conducts rapid real-time attribution analyses of extreme weather events — published its assessment of Europe's late-June 2026 heatwave, finding unequivocally that fossil fuel emissions are responsible for the event's severity: extreme heat of the June 2026 magnitude is now 10–100 times more likely than it was in the pre-industrial climate, and would have been "virtually impossible" at all without human-induced warming at its current level of approximately 1.4°C. The study found that 327 million people were exposed to heat intensified by climate change during the late-June episode, with $15.6 trillion in economic assets at risk — a ClimaMeter analysis integrated into the WWA findings showed 81% of those people and 86% of those assets were exposed to the highest category of heat. France recorded 2,025 excess deaths attributed to the June heatwave. In a parallel Nature commentary, scientists described the 2026 European heatwave as evidence that "Europe has changed irrevocably, climate-wise" — a phrase that echoes the C3S director's earlier statement about ocean temperatures entering "uncharted territory." The rapidity of the attribution (published within two weeks of the event peak) reflects the maturation of event attribution science into an operational capacity.
  • Bloomberg EU plans slower CO₂ cuts and more free permits for heavy industry in July 17 carbon market overhaul — €6B in additional free allowances, new €30B ETS Investment Booster fund; Commission seeks balance between competitiveness and decarbonisation Published July 8, 2026: Bloomberg's reporting on the upcoming EU ETS review — due to be unveiled publicly by the European Commission on July 17 — reveals the broad shape of the reform: the Commission will propose extending the period in which companies can emit CO₂ into the 2040s (a slower reduction trajectory than the current 2030 linear pathway), granting more free CO₂ allowances to energy-intensive industries in exchange for those companies committing to invest in European decarbonisation, and fast-tracking a revision of the 2026–2030 carbon benchmarks to deliver an additional €6 billion ($6.8 billion) in free allowances. The Commission will also launch the ETS Investment Booster, a new €30 billion fund based on 400 million allowances that will grant credits to eligible companies on a first-come, first-served basis for three years beginning in 2027. The policy design reflects intense lobbying pressure from heavy industry groups who argue the current ETS carbon price (hovering near €50–55/tonne in July 2026) is undermining European industrial competitiveness against non-carbon-priced rivals, particularly given the parallel US OBBBA elimination of clean energy tax credits. Climate advocates have argued the concessions undermine the ETS's primary purpose as the world's largest carbon price signal; the reform also arrives as the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism is moving toward full implementation, adding a new layer of complexity to the free-allowance architecture.
  • MSN / Carbon Brief UK heatwave forced week-long shutdown of Cambridge's Dawn AI supercomputer — facility still offline in early July; climate modeling and cancer research disrupted; illustrates growing infrastructure vulnerability to extreme heat Reported in Carbon Brief's early July 2026 daily brief (headline: "Thousands flee from wildfires | Heatwave shuts AI supercomputer | Cuban blackout"): The University of Cambridge's Dawn supercomputer — the UK's most powerful publicly funded AI research system, a £270 million facility used by institutions including the British Antarctic Survey for climate science, as well as by cancer research teams screening billions of molecules for neurodegenerative disease treatments — was forced into an abrupt shutdown after ambient temperatures at its facility exceeded 30°C during the June 27 UK heatwave and pushed internal infrastructure beyond operational safety thresholds. A Cambridge spokesperson cited "technical issues during the hot weather." The system remained offline into early July — a week-long disruption to research that cannot be paused or checkpointed cleanly without losing computational state. The incident illustrates a systemic vulnerability increasingly relevant to the intersection of weather, climate, and AI: data centres and supercomputing facilities, whose cooling infrastructure was typically engineered to historical climate norms, are now periodically exceeding design temperatures during heatwaves that are, according to the WWA study above, 10–100 times more likely than they were 50 years ago. The Dawn incident adds a concrete infrastructure dimension to the abstract climate attribution statistics.

Germany, Austria, the Czech Republic, Belgium, and the Netherlands are the primary populations now coming into the heat ridge's influence; these countries, being less adapted to extreme heat than Iberia in terms of building stock, emergency protocols, and public health preparedness, face elevated risks.   • Atlantic Basin — QUIET: CSU Downgrades 2026 Season Forecast to Fewest Storms Since 2014 NO ACTIVE THREATS; SEASON OUTLOOK REVISED DOWNWARD: No active tropical cyclones in the Atlantic or Eastern Pacific as of July 9; Colorado State University updated its seasonal forecast July 8, now calling for just 9 named storms, 4 hurricanes, and 1 major hurricane — the fewest named storms forecast in any Atlantic season since 2014, fewest hurricanes since 2015, fewest major hurricanes since 2013; El Niño odds of reaching the "very strong" category (the highest official classification) jumped from 37% to 63% since CSU's prior June forecast update; upper-level wind shear over the Atlantic main development region remains above-average, suppressing tropical cyclogenesis; no development expected in either basin through end of July Colorado State University's July 8, 2026, mid-season forecast update represents a substantial downward revision from its earlier forecasts, driven by the rapidly strengthening El Niño whose central Pacific SST anomaly has now crossed the threshold into "strong" territory and is showing 63% odds of reaching the highest "very strong" category — a level not reached in the current climate record outside of the exceptional 2015–16 event. A strong-to-very-strong El Niño produces elevated upper-level westerly wind shear over the Atlantic main development region, which mechanically disrupts tropical cyclone formation and intensification; this is the same mechanism that explains the stark contrast between the exceptionally active Western Pacific season (Super Typhoon Bavi reached Category 5 in early July) and the quiet Atlantic basin. CSU's forecast of just 9 named storms would be the lowest since 2014's 8 named storms and significantly below the 10-year average of approximately 14 named storms; however, the agency emphasises that below-normal seasons are not "no-risk" seasons — a single major landfalling hurricane generates catastrophic impacts regardless of seasonal totals, and the climatologically most active window (August 10–October 10) remains more than a month away.

Renewables

Germany dominated the renewables news cycle this week with a landmark H1 2026 result: Fraunhofer ISE analysis published July 8 shows 61.8% of Germany's net public electricity came from renewable sources in the first six months of the year, with solar hitting an all-time H1 record of 43.2 TWh (+10% year-on-year) and offshore wind reaching a record 14.6 TWh (+28%); grid-scale battery storage capacity grew from 25.4 to 29.3 GWh in the same six-month window, with more large-scale systems commissioned in H1 2026 than in all of 2025. The July 7–9 window was otherwise relatively thin for new renewables-specific stories — the major new policy signal, the EU carbon market reform previewed July 8, is covered under Climate and will have significant second-order implications for European renewable energy investment through the new ETS Investment Booster mechanism.

  • Fraunhofer ISE / SolarQuarter Germany's renewable electricity share reaches 61.8% of net public generation in H1 2026 — offshore wind record 14.6 TWh (+28%), solar all-time H1 record 43.2 TWh (+10%); battery storage capacity grows from 25.4 to 29.3 GWh in six months; zero and negative electricity price hours multiply Published July 8, 2026: Fraunhofer ISE's H1 2026 analysis of German public electricity generation shows that renewables have now crossed the threshold of generating more than three-fifths of net public electricity even including the traditionally lower-generation winter months: wind power increased total generation by 12.2% year-on-year (offshore rising from 11.4 to 14.6 TWh, onshore from 48.7 to 52.8 TWh), while solar PV reached a new first-half record at 43.2 TWh; combined, wind and solar alone generated approximately 111 TWh in the first six months of 2026. The 61.8% figure applies to net public electricity generation (excluding self-consumption and small-scale production), while the ZSW/BDEW industry association's parallel preliminary estimate — using a different denominator that includes total national consumption — puts the renewables share at 58%, up three percentage points from H1 2025; both figures represent historic milestones. A structural consequence of this renewable share is the multiplication of hours in which day-ahead electricity prices approach or fall below zero as generation routinely exceeds demand during favourable wind or solar conditions. Germany added 8.3 GW of solar PV and 3.4 GW of wind (2.5 GW onshore, 0.9 GW offshore) in H1 2026 alone, and its 10 GW/year onshore wind installation target — if sustained — puts the country on a credible path to its 80% renewable electricity goal by 2030.
  • PV Tech German solar PV generation up 10% year-on-year in H1 2026 — 8.3 GW of new capacity added in six months; total installed now 124.9 GW; on course for 80% renewable electricity target by 2030 if pace holds PV Tech's commentary on the Fraunhofer ISE H1 2026 data highlights the solar PV capacity addition trajectory: Germany added 8.3 GW of new solar PV capacity in the first six months of 2026, bringing total installed capacity from approximately 118 GW at end-2025 to 124.9 GW — putting the country on course to hit 160 GW by 2030 if the H1 run-rate is maintained, ahead of the level needed to hit 80% renewable electricity on current load growth assumptions. The 10% year-on-year increase in solar generation (from approximately 39.3 TWh to 43.2 TWh) reflects both capacity additions and somewhat better irradiance conditions compared with H1 2025; the Fraunhofer ISE's "Energy Charts" data tool shows that Germany set new single-day and single-week solar generation records during the June 2026 heatwave, when clear skies and high irradiance coincided with peak installed capacity. The concurrent growth in grid-scale battery storage (from 25.4 to 29.3 GWh in H1 alone, commissioning more capacity in six months than in all of 2025) points to the market beginning to absorb the flexibility requirements that a 60%+ renewable share creates.

Baltic Storage Platform Completes 200 MW/400 MWh Hertz Battery Hub in Estonia — Hertz 2 Commissioned July 7 on Merchant Terms Without Subsidies

A joint venture of Estonian energy developer Evecon, French independent power producer Corsica Sole, and Paris-based infrastructure fund Mirova has commissioned Hertz 2, a 100 MW/200 MWh battery energy storage system in Aruküla, Harju County, Estonia, formally opened July 7, 2026 — completing the two-project Hertz battery hub alongside its sister facility Hertz 1 at a combined 200 MW and 400 MWh capacity. Both systems are connected to the grid of Estonian transmission system operator Elering and will provide fast-response frequency regulation services alongside day-ahead and intraday electricity market participation, addressing a strategically critical gap as Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania finalise desynchronisation from the Russian/Belarusian power system and full integration with the continental European grid. The €170 million investment was made entirely with private capital and without direct subsidies, financed through an €85.6 million loan facility provided jointly by the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development, the Nordic Investment Bank, and Edmond de Rothschild Asset Management. Evecon CEO Karl-Joonatan Kvell described the subsidy-free investment decision as a signal that grid-scale battery storage is commercially viable on merchant terms in Baltic electricity markets — a proof point with implications for BESS project pipelines across the broader Nordic-Baltic region.
