Alsym Energy and ERITY Sign World's Largest Sodium-Ion Battery Agreement for Mining: 9 GWh to Replace Diesel Across Four Countries

Norwegian battery deployment platform ERITY and Woburn, Massachusetts-based Alsym Energy have signed a Strategic Relationship Agreement to deploy 9 GWh of sodium-ion battery energy storage systems across mining sites in Tanzania, Saudi Arabia, Finland, and the United States — described as the largest sodium-ion agreement in the history of the mining sector. Alsym's non-flammable, water-based sodium-ion chemistry eliminates the thermal runaway risk inherent in lithium-ion batteries, a critical safety distinction at remote mining sites where emergency response capability is limited and the consequences of a fire incident would be severe and costly. The partnership targets direct diesel displacement: grid-scale BESS co-located with on-site solar allows mining operations to dramatically reduce diesel generator run hours, with estimated energy cost reductions of 30–50% per site. Mining consumes an estimated 9% of global diesel production annually, making it one of the largest untapped markets for stationary battery storage outside the electricity sector.

Vena Energy Closes AU$1.4 Billion Green Financing for 614 MW Solar and 1.14 GWh BESS Portfolio Across Three Australian States

Singapore-headquartered Vena Energy has closed AU$1.4 billion (approximately US$920 million) in green-labelled project financing from an 11-bank consortium to develop, build, and operate six renewable energy projects across South Australia, Queensland, and New South Wales — a combined 614 MW of solar PV generation and 1.14 GWh of co-located battery energy storage. The 11-bank consortium, which includes major Australian and international infrastructure lenders, reflects growing institutional appetite for long-dated renewable energy credit exposure in a market undergoing rapid grid transformation. The portfolio is designed with co-located storage to allow solar generation to charge batteries that then dispatch into the afternoon and evening price peaks created by midday solar surpluses — a configuration that has proved highly profitable in South Australia's grid, where midday prices regularly go negative and evening dispatch windows exceed A$300/MWh. Projects are expected to reach commercial operations between 2026 and 2028.
