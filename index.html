Climate

The headline climate-health finding of the July 13–16 window is EuroMOMO data published July 13 by Euronews and multiple outlets: 10,650 excess deaths were recorded across 27 European countries during the single peak week of June 22–28, 2026 — when the first European heatwave reached its maximum intensity — with France and Belgium both logging "very high excess" mortality, the highest category on the EuroMOMO scale. Belgium's excess mortality was the highest recorded during any heatwave since EuroMOMO records began in 2000. The World Weather Attribution group separately confirmed this heatwave would have been "virtually impossible" without human-caused climate change, reinforcing the direct attribution line from global warming to what is now the worst heat-mortality event in European records. No new Copernicus or CAMS scientific bulletins have been published in this 72-hour window; the June 2026 baselines established in the July 14 digest remain operative: June ranked second-warmest June globally (+1.39°C above pre-industrial); 82% of the global ocean in marine heatwave at month's end.

The week of June 22–28 represents the peak of the first of what became four successive European heat events in summer 2026. The total of 10,650 excess deaths in a single week from all causes — with scientists confirming no other major confounding factor (such as COVID-19) that week — is the largest single-week heat-attributable excess mortality figure in the EuroMOMO record and exceeds the weekly peak of the 2003 European heatwave (which killed approximately 70,000 people over the full summer but whose peak-week mortality, while enormous, was not systematically documented at this level of cross-national precision). The "very high" designation — the highest of four EuroMOMO excess mortality categories — is rarely applied; only France and Belgium reached this threshold during the June event. France's excess mortality reflects the overlap of extreme temperatures with a heat-vulnerable elderly population in urban areas poorly adapted to air conditioning; Belgium's being the highest on record underscores how quickly European countries that had previously escaped the worst heatwaves are now facing mortality consequences comparable to the Mediterranean nations. WWA's rapid attribution finding — published days after the event — confirms this week would have been "virtually impossible" without anthropogenic warming, adding to an unbroken string of attribution results for every major European heatwave since 2021 that have reached the same conclusion. The second, third, and fourth waves of the 2026 European summer (July events, still ongoing) have not yet had their mortality data quantified at this level of cross-national resolution, but the fourth wave — with red alerts across six countries and temperatures approaching 45°C in France — is expected to produce a comparable or larger signal when data are compiled.

Renewables

Solar hit a landmark in European electricity markets: Ember data published July 14 confirm that solar provided exactly 25% of EU monthly electricity generation in June 2026 — the first time solar has ever supplied a full quarter of EU power in a single calendar month, making it the EU's largest single electricity source for the month, ahead of nuclear (21%), gas (15%), and wind (14%). The milestone was simultaneously a function of rapid capacity buildout and record June sunshine hours driven by the heat dome. In California, Avantus closed a $525 million construction financing package on July 13 for the Aratina 2 solar-plus-storage project in Kern County, adding 150 MW of solar and 452 MWh of battery storage under a 15-year PPA with Southern California Edison.

  • Ember / Euronews Solar provides 25% of EU electricity in June 2026 for the first time — record 52 TWh generated; solar ranked #1 source ahead of nuclear (21%), gas (15%), wind (14%), hydro (12%), coal (8%); only third month solar has been EU's top power source; Germany 36%, Spain 34%, 18 EU countries hit new monthly solar records; driven by both heatwave sunshine hours and five-year buildout (65 GW installed in 2025 alone) Published July 14, 2026 (Euronews; Ember press release July 14; CleanTechnica July 14; PV Tech; Electrek July 15; GreentechLead; Envirotec): Ember's monthly EU electricity generation tracker confirmed that solar generated 52 TWh of EU electricity in June 2026, representing exactly 25% of total EU net public electricity generation — both absolute and percentage-share records for a calendar month. The previous monthly record was solar's 47 TWh (23%) output in May 2026. The June milestone is historically significant: reaching 25% was a symbolic threshold often cited as the point at which solar transitions from a "niche" to a "dominant" generation source in policy and market analyses. For the June 2026 result, two distinct factors reinforced each other: the structural factor (a cumulative buildout of approximately 65 GW of new EU solar capacity in 2025 alone, extending a trend that has seen EU solar grow by more than a fifth per year every year since 2021) and the situational factor (the June 2026 European heatwave brought anomalously high solar irradiance and record sunshine hours across central and southern Europe, boosting output from existing capacity above typical June levels). Spain's 34% share (first time Spain's monthly solar fraction exceeded one-third) and Germany's 36% share in June (building on the 33% May record) led the EU member state ranking; Poland's recent offshore and utility-scale buildout put it among the fastest-improving markets. Nuclear's second-place 21% share in June — typically the largest single EU electricity source in non-peak-solar months — was suppressed partly by the same heatwave that boosted solar, through the river-cooling constraints that took French reactors offline or at reduced output. The combination of rapidly falling solar LCOE, a pro-deployment regulatory environment in most EU member states, and the heatwave-driven impetus from France's nuclear curtailment crisis is expected to sustain EU solar's growth trajectory well into the 2030s.

Google and Cypress Creek Energy Break Ground on America's Largest Solar Project — 2.5 GW / 2.9 GWh Steel River Energy Center in Arkansas

Renewables developer Cypress Creek Energy and Google broke ground on July 14, 2026 on the first two phases of the Steel River Energy Center in Wilson, Arkansas — described as the nation's largest solar energy project to date. The initial phases will deliver 1.6 GW-DC of solar photovoltaic capacity paired with 1.9 GWh of battery storage; the project's three-phase buildout will reach a total of 2.5 GW-DC of solar and 2.9 GWh of battery storage when fully complete by 2029, sufficient to supply more than 315,000 Arkansas homes annually. Google has signed a power purchase agreement covering the first two phases, acting as the anchor corporate offtaker and signalling its continued strategy of securing large-scale, regionally sited clean power to meet the electricity demand of its AI data centre expansion. For Cypress Creek — a North Carolina-based developer with one of the largest US distributed solar portfolios — Steel River represents its entry into the utility-scale gigawatt class and its largest single project to date.

The Weather Company Partners With Redfin to Embed Climate and Weather Data Into Every US Home Listing

The Weather Company — operator of The Weather Channel app and weather.com — announced on July 15, 2026 that it has signed an exclusive partnership with real estate platform Redfin to make ZIP code-level historical weather metrics available on every for-sale home listing on Redfin.com and the Redfin iOS and Android apps. Buyers browsing listings can now view average annual temperature, precipitation, snowfall, humidity, and UV index for each property's ZIP code, with the integration designed to address what a concurrent Redfin survey (Ipsos, May 2026; 4,000 US respondents) identified as the top two drivers of out-of-state relocation decisions: better weather (22% of movers) and lower climate/disaster risk (21%). The partnership expands The Weather Company's commercial data footprint beyond media and enterprise applications into the US residential real estate market, where climate risk analytics has been a rapidly growing product category — driven by Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and FHFA climate risk disclosure expectations and by the increasing salience of wildfire, flood, and hurricane risk to home buyers in exposed markets.
