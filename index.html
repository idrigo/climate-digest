Climate

The week's most counterintuitive climate finding comes from Copernicus CAMS: global biomass burning carbon emissions for the first half of 2026 were the lowest on record in the 24-year satellite series — a result driven entirely by reduced fire activity in tropical Africa and Asia — yet the late-June and early-July surges in North America and Europe are already erasing that positive lead and may push 2026's full-year total toward the top of the historical range. Separately, C3S confirmed that global ocean surface temperatures have remained at all-time daily records every single day since June 21, when the daily mean first hit 20.86°C; C3S director Carlo Buontempo warned on July 7 that the sustained anomaly may signal "the beginning of a new phase, leading, once more, to uncharted territory" — a phrase that carries particular weight as the strong El Niño fingerprint deepens in the central Pacific.

  • Copernicus CAMS Lowest global biomass burning emissions on record during first half of 2026 — sub-400 megatonnes carbon January–June, lowest level in the 24-year CAMS GFAS satellite dataset; reduced African and Asian savanna fires the primary driver; late-June North American and European surges now threatening to eliminate the positive first-half result Published July 6, 2026: The Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service confirmed that total global carbon emissions from biomass burning remained below 400 megatonnes of carbon for the entire first half of 2026 — the lowest level recorded in the CAMS Global Fire Assimilation System dataset since its 2003 origin. The dominant factor is a structural reduction in fire activity across tropical Africa, historically the world's single largest biomass-burning region, as shifts in land use and seasonal rainfall patterns have suppressed savanna burning; Asia was the second-largest contributing region at approximately 113 Mt C. The counterintuitive implication for this digest's primary story — the European and Colorado fire emergencies — is one of scale and geography: the regional extremes that have dominated headlines represent a large proportional departure from European and North American baselines but are still a fraction of the total suppression achieved globally by African and Asian fire reduction; however, CAMS scientists note explicitly that the late-June ignition of large wildfires across North America and Eurasia has "quickly increased" the global total, and the final 2026 full-year figure will depend heavily on whether fire season intensity in the Northern Hemisphere's remainder of summer can be contained.
  • Carbon Brief / C3S Global ocean SST at all-time record 20.86°C on June 21 — staying at daily record levels every day since; C3S director warns of "beginning of a new phase" toward "uncharted territory" as El Niño-amplified marine heat sets a new baseline for global temperature anomalies Published July 7, 2026: Carbon Brief's July 7 daily digest synthesises the latest Copernicus Climate Change Service data showing that daily global mean sea-surface temperature — excluding polar regions — first exceeded the previous all-time daily record on June 21, reaching 20.86°C and surpassing the 20.83°C recorded on the same calendar day in both 2023 and 2024. Critically, CAMS monitoring has confirmed that SST has remained at or above the daily all-time record level every single day since June 21 — a sustained record streak without historical precedent in the satellite era. C3S director Carlo Buontempo characterised the situation as potentially "the beginning of a new phase, leading, once more, to uncharted territory," echoing similar language used at the start of the 2023–24 anomalous SST warming episode that preceded the current El Niño onset; the difference now is that the El Niño is already established and strengthening toward the "strong" threshold, suggesting the marine heat anomaly may persist and intensify rather than revert to mean; elevated SST in the western Pacific and tropical Atlantic also acts as a direct thermodynamic fuel source for the intensification of systems such as Typhoon Bavi and any future Atlantic storms, making this metric particularly relevant for the tropical cyclone season.

  • Western Pacific — Super Typhoon Bavi (PAGASA name: Inday) — NEW MAJOR EVENT ENTERING PHILIPPINES: Entered Philippine Area of Responsibility July 8 at 03:30 UTC; current intensity Category 3–4 equivalent — 185 kph (115 mph) sustained winds, gusts to 230 kph, down from Category 5 peak of approximately 290 kph; PAGASA Wind Signal No. 3 hoisted for Batanes, Babuyan Islands, and northern Cagayan; Signal No. 2 for Apayao and northern portions of Isabela; direct landfall in the Philippine main islands not in the forecast track — centre expected to pass through the Batanes area then approach Taiwan around July 11–12 before tracking toward southeastern China; gale warning in force for northern and eastern Philippine seaboards; this event was not in the July 7 digest Super Typhoon Bavi — the Western Pacific's most significant tropical cyclone of the 2026 season to date — made a catastrophic direct hit on the US territory of Rota, part of the Northern Mariana Islands, on July 6 with maximum sustained winds of approximately 180 mph (290 kph), equivalent to a peak Category 5 hurricane. The mayor of Rota, Aubry Hocog, reported that the majority of homes on the island were damaged or destroyed, with damage to public facilities, government assets, private residences, the airport, and the seaport; all approximately 2,000 residents were left without power or running water. Guam, south of Rota, and Saipan and Tinian, north of Rota, fared substantially better as the eye tracked directly over the smaller island. The storm has since undergone an eyewall replacement cycle and weakened from its Category 5 peak, entering the Philippine Area of Responsibility on July 8 as a powerful but no longer extreme storm. PAGASA's forecast track keeps the centre north of Luzon's main island, with the Batanes island group — the Philippines' northernmost islands — bearing the brunt of typhoon-force winds. The typhoon is expected to bring heavy rainfall to northern Luzon, Cagayan, and the Babuyan Islands over July 8–10, with a secondary landfall risk in Taiwan and southeastern China emerging as the dominant concern beyond July 11.

The additional 2,000 evacuees (bringing the total to 12,000) were added overnight as crews undertook precautionary expansions of evacuation perimeters on the fire's western flank; the evacuation zone covers more than a dozen small towns and villages in the foothills of the French Pyrenees across a roughly 40 km perimeter. EU reinforcements remain on scene: Canadair water-bombing aircraft from Cyprus and Sweden dispatched under the EU Civil Protection Mechanism continue to support the 700 French firefighters. The Tour de France disruption continues, with spectators barred from Stage 4 sections in the affected zone; race organisers are monitoring forecasts for Stages 5 and 6 as the pyrenean route passes through the region. Full containment is not expected within 48 hours, but the stabilisation is the most significant operational improvement since the fire began.

The strong El Niño now tracking near 2.0°C central Pacific SST anomaly is producing above-average upper-level westerly wind shear over the Atlantic main development region, suppressing tropical cyclone formation; NOAA's seasonal forecast (55% probability of a below-normal season) remains the best estimate for the rest of the season, with the climatologically most active window (August 10 – October 10) still more than a month away. The Western Pacific, by contrast, has produced an exceptionally early super typhoon in Bavi, consistent with the warm SST anomalies documented in the C3S data above.

Offshore wind's transitional-depth foundation gap attracted a significant new entrant this week with the launch of Neretek, a joint venture between UK engineering firm Wood Thilsted and Copenhagen Offshore Partners affiliate COP Frontier, targeting fixed-bottom foundations in the 60–90 metre water depth range that currently sits between proven monopile technology and unproven floating wind — with industry veteran Jonathan Cole named executive chair. Neretek Launches as Deepwater Offshore Wind Foundation Company — Wood Thilsted and COP Frontier Form JV to Commercialise BC90 Technology; Jonathan Cole Named Executive Chair

UK structural engineering firm Wood Thilsted and COP Frontier, an affiliate of Copenhagen-based offshore wind investor Copenhagen Offshore Partners (COP), have launched Neretek — a new company focused on commercialising fixed-bottom offshore wind foundations designed for water depths of 60–90 metres. The venture's centrepiece technology is the BC90™ foundation, which combines design principles from existing monopile and jacket foundation families to create a solution deployable using current port infrastructure and local supply chains, without requiring the purpose-built installation vessels that have constrained floating wind deployment. Jonathan Cole, a widely recognised offshore wind industry veteran and former chief executive of Mainstream Renewable Power, has been named executive chair of Neretek. Cole has described the BC90 technology as "capable of removing the potential hiatus between existing fixed offshore wind designs and yet-to-be-commercialised floating offshore wind" — a gap that is commercially critical for the UK's emerging Other Deepwater Offshore Wind category and equivalent zones in the North Sea, Irish Sea, and Mediterranean.

Octopus Energy Enters European Home Battery Storage Market with Plug-In and Wall-Mounted Systems Across Five Countries

UK-based integrated energy supplier and clean energy unicorn Octopus Energy has announced its entry into the European residential battery storage market with two hardware products: a 2 kWh plug-in battery system designed for apartment dwellers or smaller homes without wall-mounting capability, and a 5 kWh wall-mounted system for standard residential installation. Launches are planned from 2027 across five European markets, with Italy announced as the first country in the rollout. The move marks a significant expansion of Octopus Energy's business model beyond electricity retail and tariff products (including its widely cited Agile and Intelligent Octopus offerings) into hardware ownership, positioning it in direct competition with dedicated home storage manufacturers such as SONNEN and Tesla Energy in the European market. With approximately 8 million customer accounts and proprietary smart-charging software already integrated into its energy platform, Octopus is betting that its existing customer relationships and grid-interaction software will differentiate its battery products from commodity hardware alternatives.
