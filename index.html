Industry News

Trump Administration Accelerates Offshore Wind Dismantlement — $2.5B+ Paid, California Fights Back

The U.S. Interior Department struck its eighth offshore wind lease buyout deal this month, agreeing to pay Invenergy $765 million to surrender four early-stage leases in the New York Bight, Gulf of Maine, and Morro Bay — projects that had already been cancelled due to economic and regulatory pressure. Drawn from the Treasury's Judgment Fund rather than Interior's budget, the payments bring the administration's total offshore wind lease buyback programme to more than $2.5 billion. Invenergy said it will redirect the proceeds into natural gas plants across five Midwest states and geothermal projects in the western US. The accelerating buyout strategy drew an immediate legal challenge: California Attorney General Rob Bonta and the California Energy Commission filed a notice of intent to sue Interior and developer Golden State Wind over a separate cancellation they say puts more than $100 million in voter-approved clean energy funds at risk.

Weather Trading Gains Traction as Wind and Solar Reshape Power Markets

As wind and solar capacity expands across US and European grids, power markets are becoming increasingly exposed to short-term weather swings — fuelling rapid growth in weather-linked trading strategies and hedging products. Utilities, energy traders, and renewable developers are turning to weather derivatives, volumetric contracts, and AI-driven short-range forecasting tools to manage the price volatility that comes with a weather-dependent generation mix. The trend is opening new market opportunities for specialised weather analytics and forecasting firms, which are increasingly positioning their products directly to energy desk clients rather than only to traditional meteorology customers.

NextEra Energy's $67B Dominion Takeover Advances Through Regulatory Review

The all-stock merger between NextEra Energy and Dominion Energy — announced in May and valued at approximately $67 billion — continues to move through a 12–18-month regulatory review process. The deal would create the world's largest regulated electric utility, serving roughly 10 million customers across Florida, Virginia, North Carolina, and South Carolina and holding a 110 GW combined generation portfolio. A key strategic asset is Dominion's 2.6 GW Coastal Virginia Offshore Wind project — the largest offshore wind development in the United States — which began generating first power in March 2026 and is on track for full commissioning in early 2027. Industry observers note the merger positions the combined entity to capitalise on surging electricity demand from AI data centres expanding across the US Southeast.
